INTRODUCTION:

Agile’s flexibility has spawned AgileX—custom frameworks varying by organization. This adaptability creates inefficiency: each company’s version differs, from heavy planning to minimal structure. Teams face learning curves, processes don’t align, and collaboration suffers. The noisy landscape dilutes Agile’s promise of streamlined work.

THESIS:

Kanban-Zai is a pragmatic, common-sense approach to collaboration—easy to explain in minutes yet powerful enough to support complex workflows. Built on four core principles, it can be used to create a new way of working or to clearly define and improve existing collaboration, without pointless bureaucracy.

What is Culture?

Culture is the framework upon which we rely when our next steps are uncertain. It emerges as the guiding force in times of ambiguity—driving productivity, shaping collaboration, and retaining a distinctive character that cannot be replicated or remain static. At its core, culture comprises the shared values, beliefs, behaviours, customs, and artefacts that define a group or society. It moulds the way we perceive the world, interact with one another, and organise our lives, imparting a sense of what is acceptable, meaningful, and valuable within our community.

Culture exists on a spectrum. At one end, there are rigid, bureaucratic cultures, marked by formal hierarchies, standardised procedures, and top-down decision-making. At the opposite end lie organic, adaptive, and innovative cultures—such as agile, adhocratic, or pragmatic cultures—that prioritise collaboration, flexibility, and bottom-up creativity. For instance, within a pragmatic culture, common sense and practical outcomes guide decision-making. Norms are adopted only when necessary to resolve uncertainty, thus fostering rapid adaptation and creative problem-solving—a stark contrast to bureaucratic systems, where pre-established rules dictate actions regardless of context.

Purpose of This Artefact

This artefact is designed to resolve uncertainty by establishing a predefined consensus—Team Norms—anchored in common sense. Norms are adopted only when common sense alone cannot drive progress. This approach forms the backbone of effective collaboration and offers essential guidance during periods of ambiguity. Whether you are constructing a boardroom governance structure, instituting shareholder or CEO oversight, or coordinating among product owners, teams, or technical leaders, this artefact—when combined with the Values Artefact—enables Kanban-Zai to operate as either a standalone framework or as part of a larger hierarchical system.

Hierarchies naturally emerge as we organise; however, their success depends heavily on the internal culture of each nested team. Although the adage “good soldiers follow orders” may hold in certain contexts, larger hierarchies often require a degree of autonomy at the periphery, as ground-level realities can diverge from strategic vision.

Practical Examples

1. Refining an Action Plan:
A guideline such as “Take the top card—do not cherry-pick” ensures fairness and consistency in work allocation, even when the need for adjustments is not immediately apparent.

2. Accidental Preference and Hero Culture:
Occasionally, an individual may achieve an outcome that was not explicitly requested but significantly benefits the group. This “accidental preference” can lead to a hero culture—a system that celebrates exceptional individuals for extraordinary problem-solving. Although hero culture can inspire short-term motivation, over-reliance on individual heroics may foster unsustainable expectations, create dependency on a few key figures, and undermine collective responsibility. In a well-balanced organisation, while individual excellence is acknowledged, the focus remains on building resilient, team-driven systems.

3. Organisational versus Team Culture:
In large organisations, each team cultivates its own distinct culture. However, conflicts may arise when the overall organisational culture diverges from that of an individual team. For example, a team with a longstanding, effective collaboration with an external entity might find that relationship disrupted by a misaligned organisational culture. In such cases, a well-crafted team norm can help to bridge the disconnect.

Guidance for Creating Team Norms

Embracing Pragmatism with Kanban-Zai
Kanban-Zai champions a pragmatic, common-sense culture that avoids the needless proliferation of norms. Although past experiences may provide familiar templates, every team’s culture is unique and demands adaptive solutions. Acknowledging that outcomes can be unpredictable and recognising that common sense may occasionally fall short, Kanban-Zai is designed to resolve dilemmas effectively when common sense alone is insufficient. While it is possible to construct a complex bureaucratic system by codifying every detail, such an approach is resource-intensive and tends to escalate in complexity over time. Our default stance is pragmatism—introducing norms only when they are truly necessary.

Key Principles for Effective Norm Creation:

* Avoid Overregulation:
Introduce norms solely to address genuine gaps in common sense or clarity, and retire them when they no longer serve a purpose. Overregulation—often driven by assumptions or outdated practices—can lead to conflicting rules that develop into a cumbersome bureaucratic maze.

* Develop Dynamic Norms for Clarity:
Establish clear, adaptable guidelines that address the inherent ambiguities in team interactions. These evolving norms should align with the organisation’s changing needs, ensuring consistent and transparent decision-making even in complex situations. Although the concept appears straightforward, its practical implementation requires continuous, thoughtful effort.

* Maintain a Living Record of Progress:
Document the evolution of your team norms as a timeline of resolved uncertainties. This living record not only reflects your team’s journey towards alignment and effective complexity management, but also serves as a valuable reference for addressing future challenges.

* Foster Internal Self-Governance:
Ensure that team norms are developed and maintained internally rather than imposed from external sources. This internal ownership fosters authenticity and guarantees that the norms genuinely reflect the team’s unique dynamics and collective wisdom.

Measurement

Empirically, a team’s rolling average velocity is often inversely proportional to the complexity of its norms—simpler norms generally facilitate swifter progress. While this trade-off might be desirable in certain contexts, excessive normative complexity tends to be counterproductive. (Empirical validation of this correlation is required.)

How Are Team Norms Implemented?

The simplest method of implementation is to print the norms and display them prominently where everyone can see. It is imperative that everyone is aware of them, and that there is a shared understanding which extends indefinitely—that is, everyone knows that everyone knows, ad infinitum.

By adhering to these guidelines, teams can cultivate a pragmatic culture that leverages common sense, adapts to uncertainty, and builds resilient, collaborative systems.

ARTEFACT 4: VALUES – (Equates to the self-orientation variable of the trust equation). INTER-TEAM COMMS. Morals, perception, ethos

Purpose:
Our values define who we are as a team and set clear boundaries for our behaviour—both internally and when engaging with external stakeholders. Think of them as our team's interface: they’re the expectations we live by, and they’re designed to be measurable and actionable.

Our Core Principles:
This artefact captures our essential principles in a way that drives real action. Instead of relying on abstract ideas like “integrity” or “teamwork,” our values are expressed in concrete, specific terms. They show not only how we operate day-to-day but also who we truly are.

Clarity, Measurability, and Accountability:
For our values to have an impact, they must be clear and measurable. Vague slogans like “We work to the beat of ‘xyz unknown philosophy’” don’t help us steer our actions. Instead, our values spell out exactly what we do. For example:

* “We prioritize all external requests using a defined, transparent set of criteria.”

* “Our lead times and response times are set to ensure customer needs are met promptly.”

* “In a crisis, we follow a predetermined protocol to manage and resolve issues quickly.”

* “We define the formats for information exchange, the tools we use, and the kinds of work we excel at.”

When our values are defined in measurable terms, they give us a practical way to evaluate our actions. Any deviation from these standards signals that we might be shifting toward self-interest, which can erode trust.

SMART Values – A Practical Approach:
One powerful way to operationalize our values is by using a SMART card format. With this approach, each value is:

* Specific: Clearly defining what it means in practice.

* Measurable: Establishing criteria to know when we're meeting it.

* Achievable: Realistic within our team's context.

* Relevant: Directly linked to our overall goals and customer needs—remember, our “customer” might be the team lead, the CEO, or any key stakeholder whose requirements we aim to satisfy.

* Time-bound: Tied to clear review periods or triggers for action.

Examples of Measurable Value Systems:
To integrate our values into everyday practice, we might use a dynamic, tiered system like a traffic light model:

* Green Light: Normal operations, where standard processes apply.

* Orange Light: A signal for elevated urgency, indicating that we need to focus more and commit extra time or resources.

* Red Light: A critical state where exceptional measures are required, prompting the team to immediately activate crisis protocols.

This traffic light system is just one way to translate our values into actionable steps that everyone can understand and measure.

Conclusion:
By defining our values in specific, measurable terms—and by using a SMART card approach—we build a robust framework that clearly establishes our identity and sets precise expectations for behavior. This ensures that we stay aligned with our internal standards and customer needs, minimizes self-oriented actions, and ultimately builds the trust and accountability essential for our success.

Measurement and Adaptability

A key strength of the Kanban-Zai framework is its emphasis on measurement. Each artefact is not just a descriptive tool but a measurable object in its own right—allowing teams to build models that are both objective and adaptable. By embedding metrics directly into the framework, organisations can:

* Evaluate Alignment: Assess whether the strategic vision is still fit for purpose based on predefined outcome metrics.

* Track Execution: Monitor team velocity, cycle time, and work-item transitions through clearly defined states.

* Refine Culture: Measure the impact of team norms on collaborative efficiency and identify areas where bureaucratic overhead may be impeding progress.

* Validate Values: Ensure that values translate into consistent, customer-centric behaviour by quantifying deviations and adherence through systems such as the traffic light model.

This rigorous, measurement-focused approach allows for objective discussions about team models. Because all models are built solely using these four core artefacts, changes can be proposed, tested, and validated without disruptive overhauls—providing a trusted framework that evolves in step with the team’s needs.

Conclusion

The Kanban-Zai core artefacts provide more than just a set of guidelines; they offer a comprehensive, measurable framework that aligns strategy, execution, culture, and values. This integration not only enhances transparency and trust but also creates a dynamic environment where teams can adapt quickly to changes while maintaining a clear focus on customer needs and strategic outcomes. With these artefacts at its core, organisations can objectively evaluate and adjust their team models—ensuring continuous improvement and sustainable success.
